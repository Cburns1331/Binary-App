Can you have a negative number in binary?

Congratulations! You've learned the basics of counting and converting with binary numbers!

So, what's next?

The logical step now would be to address the idea of negative numbers. True, you could just slap a "-" in front of a binary digit and to you that might make sense, but to a computer not so much. How are you supposed to indicate "-" when all you have is on (1) or off (0) to work with?

The binary numbers we've worked with so far have been what we call "unsigned", meaning they are all positive. To represent negative numbers, we have to use "signed" binary numbers. There are three different forms of signed binary: signed, one's complement, and two's complement.

The first form is just called "signed". This form of binary says to take the leftmost bit as the sign bit, and not as a number. A leftmost bit of 0 means the number is positive and 1 means the number is negative.

Notice that in the last row we had to add a preceding digit to indicate the sign. You can only do this if you are not limited to a certain number of bits (digits) in the binary number. For example, if you only had 8 bits (8 digits, called a byte) to work with and your unsigned number was 10000001, then you would not be able to represent it in signed form. However, this makes it easy to know if a number is negative or not if you know it is in signed form, as you can just look at the first bit. Signed binary is then easily changed between positive and negative by changing the sign bit (the leftmost digit) between 0 and 1.

One's complement is the next method, and it sets up the foundations for two's complement. One's complement takes the binary number and flips all of the bits (digits) to their opposite.

Notice that in the first row of binary numbers we had to add a preceding zero. This is because one's complement form has the same needs as regular signed form: the first bit always indicates the sign of the number. A 0 means it is positive and a 1 means it is negative. One's complement can also be easily switched between positive and negative by flipping all the bits. (i.e. to go from negative to positive, or positive to negative, just flip the bits).

One's complement also has a special case: there is a positive zero and a negative zero. These should usually be handled differently when they appear, which is one of the reasons one's complement is not used very often.

Two's complement extends one's complement, and it is by far the most used method. Two's complement converts an unsigned number into one's complement and then adds 1 to the entire number. This is important because the 1 might carry through the number. For example, 0100 becomes 1011 when we take the first step (converting to one's complement). Then, we add the number 1 to the entire number. Keep in mind that in binary, 1 + 1 = 0 with a carry of 1 (because there is no "2" digit). So, 1011 + 1 = 1100 because of the carry from the first and second digits.

Two's complement has the same needs as the other two: the first bit indicates sign. However, like the other two methods, two's complement can be switched between positive and negative by repeating the same process. To go from a negative number (say, 1101), you flip all the bits (0010) and add 1 (0011). And then from a positive number (0011) you flip the bits (1100) and add 1 (1101).

Two's complement is used the most in computers because of its addition and subtraction properties, but we'll cover those in a later section.
